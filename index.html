I am an evolutionary biologist studying the processes that generate species richness. My research focuses on speciation and the evolution of coexistence between close relatives, and a key aim of my work is to draw statistical generalizations about the hypothesized drivers of these two processes.

To this end, I develop new analytical tools for questions like "is ecology-based divergent selection generally required for speciation?" and "do the processes promoting coexstence tend to differ across environments?". I deploy these tools to analyze data from the field, lab, and museum specimens. I am particularly interested in diversification dynamics in non-model systems — lineages that evolve and speciate in the complex continental settings home to most of life.

Currently I'm a PhD candidate in Jason Weir's lab at the University of Toronto. I'm excited to be joining Daniel Matute's lab this summer as a postdoc at UNC, where I'll be expanding my focus into speciation genetics. With the Matute group, I will aim to tease out general processes behind the evolution of non-ephemeral, intrinsic reproductive barriers that are robust to environmental change and that are therefore critical to the build-up of lasting biodiveristy.
